Most record-keeping rules run on a five-year clock from when a return is lodged. Capital gains
tax evidence is different: the relevant clock doesn't start until the asset is disposed of,
which for a property or a long-held investment can be decades after the records were first
created.

## Why CGT evidence has a longer effective life

The general substantiation rule requires records for five years from lodgment, but a capital
gain or loss isn't calculated until an asset is sold, gifted, or otherwise disposed of, and the
five-year clock for records supporting that year's return only starts then. In practice, this
means every record affecting an asset's cost base needs to be kept for as long as the asset is
held, plus five years afterward — for a rental property bought early in an investing career,
that can mean holding onto records for well over a decade before the return they eventually
support is even lodged.

## What builds the cost base

An asset's cost base isn't just its purchase price. It generally includes incidental costs of
acquisition (stamp duty, conveyancing, agent fees), costs of owning the asset that weren't
otherwise deductible along the way, and capital costs incurred to increase or preserve the
asset's value, such as capital improvements. Each of these needs its own supporting record —
the purchase contract and settlement statement, but also every subsequent invoice for capital
work — because the total cost base directly reduces the eventual capital gain.

## Capital improvements need to be tracked separately from repairs

An amount already deducted as a repair against rental income can't also be added to the cost
base — the two are separate categories with separate records, and mixing them up either
understates a deduction along the way or double-counts a cost at disposal. Keeping capital
improvement invoices in a running, dated log as work happens — rather than trying to
reconstruct which renovations were "improvements" years later — is what actually makes this
distinction usable when a disposal eventually needs to be reported.

## Depreciation claimed reduces the cost base too

Where a Division 40 or Division 43 deduction has been claimed against a property over the years
it was held, the cumulative amount claimed generally reduces the asset's cost base for CGT
purposes at disposal, so it isn't counted twice — once as an annual deduction and again as part
of the capital loss or reduced gain at sale. Depreciation schedules are therefore CGT records
as much as they are annual-deduction records.

## What this doesn't cover

This describes the general cost-base record-keeping principle only. It doesn't cover the main
residence exemption, the CGT discount, or how cost-base rules differ for assets acquired before
CGT began — each depends on the specific asset, when it was acquired, and how it was used over
the full period it was held.
