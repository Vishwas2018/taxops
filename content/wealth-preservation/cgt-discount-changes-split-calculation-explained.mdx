Alongside the negative gearing reform, the 2026-27 Budget announced a change to the capital
gains tax (CGT) discount. It has since been legislated (the Treasury Laws Amendment (Tax Reform
No. 1) Act 2026 received Royal Assent on 26 June 2026), but the new arrangements are **not yet
in effect** - they only apply to gains accruing from 1 July 2027, and every gain realised before
then continues to be taxed exactly as it is today.

## What's changing, from 1 July 2027

The current 50% CGT discount for individuals, trusts, and partnerships is being replaced with
cost base indexation (using the Consumer Price Index, the same mechanism that applied between
1985 and 1999) plus a minimum tax rate of 30% on real capital gains. Indexation reduces a
taxable gain by the inflation component of the return rather than by a flat half, so the actual
benefit compared to today's 50% discount depends on the asset's real (after-inflation) return -
a lower real return keeps more of the current discount's benefit; a higher real return means
less of it applies. The changes apply broadly to CGT assets - property and shares alike - not
to residential property alone.

## Why gains get split into two portions, not one calculation

For an asset already held before 1 July 2027 and sold after that date, the gain isn't
recalculated wholesale under the new rules. Instead it's split at 1 July 2027:

- The **portion of the gain that accrued before 1 July 2027** continues to use the current 50%
  discount, calculated against the asset's value at that date (determined either by a valuation
  or a specified apportionment formula based on the asset's growth rate over its holding period).
- The **portion accruing from 1 July 2027 onward** is calculated using cost base indexation and
  is subject to the new minimum tax rate, using the 1 July 2027 value as the new cost base.

No tax is payable on either portion until the asset is actually sold - the split only affects
how the eventual gain is calculated at that point, not when tax is triggered.

## The minimum tax rate on the post-2027 portion

Real capital gains accruing from 1 July 2027 are subject to a minimum tax rate of 30%, which
only affects a gain otherwise taxed below that rate - it doesn't change the tax on a gain
already taxed at 30% or higher under an investor's marginal rate. Recipients of means-tested
income support payments (such as the Age Pension or JobSeeker) are exempt from the minimum tax
in a year they receive such a payment.

## What stays the same

The main residence exemption is unaffected. The four small business CGT concessions are
unchanged. Assets bought and sold entirely before 1 July 2027 use the current 50% discount in
full, with no split calculation involved. New-build residential property investors can choose
between the current 50% discount and the new arrangements when they eventually sell.

## What this doesn't cover

This describes the discount mechanism and the pre/post-2027 split only. It doesn't cover the
related negative gearing change (covered separately), how the apportionment formula for an
asset's 1 July 2027 value is actually calculated for a specific asset, or how the reform
interacts with assets acquired before 20 September 1985 (pre-CGT assets) - those depend on the
specific asset and its history.
