Superannuation contributions fall into two broad categories for tax purposes: concessional
(before-tax) and non-concessional (after-tax). Understanding which contributions are
concessional, and how the annual cap on them works, is central to planning around super without
triggering an unexpected tax outcome.

## What counts as concessional

Concessional contributions include employer Super Guarantee payments, any additional
salary-sacrificed super, and personal contributions for which a valid notice of intent to
claim a deduction has been lodged. These contributions are taxed at 15% within the fund
(rather than at the member's marginal rate) when they're received, which is the "concession"
the name refers to.

## The annual cap

There's a single annual cap that applies to the total of all concessional contributions from
every source combined — employer, salary-sacrificed, and personal deductible contributions
together, not each counted separately against its own limit. Contributions above the cap are
included in the member's assessable income and taxed at their marginal rate, with an offset for
the 15% already paid in the fund, plus an excess concessional contributions charge to account
for the tax paid later than it otherwise would have been.

## Carry-forward of unused cap

Members with a total super balance below the relevant threshold on 30 June of the prior year
may be able to carry forward unused concessional cap amounts from up to five previous financial
years. This can allow a larger concessional contribution in a single year than the standard
annual cap would otherwise permit — for example, after a year of lower income or irregular
work. Whether unused cap is available, and how much, depends on contribution history across
that five-year window.

## Division 293: a related, separate rule

Higher-income earners should be aware that concessional contributions can also trigger
Division 293 tax — an additional 15% on some or all concessional contributions where combined
income plus those contributions exceeds a separate, fixed threshold. Division 293 and the
concessional contributions cap are independent rules that can both apply to the same
contributions.

## What this doesn't cover

This article describes the concessional cap mechanism only. It doesn't cover the
non-concessional contributions cap, bring-forward arrangements for non-concessional
contributions, or how the carry-forward rule interacts with a specific member's total super
balance history — each depends on figures particular to the individual's account and
contribution history over prior years.
