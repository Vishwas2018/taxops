Division 293 is often described as "an extra tax on high income earners' super," which is
roughly right but skips the part that actually determines whether — and how much — of it
applies: a combined-income test that isn't the same as looking at salary alone.

## The combined income test

Division 293 compares a single combined figure — most of a taxpayer's other assessable income
plus their concessional super contributions for the year — against a fixed threshold. It's the
combination that's tested, not income on its own. This is the mechanism behind the "straddle"
situation TaxOps's own Division 293 calculator surfaces: someone whose income alone sits below
the threshold can still be liable if adding their concessional contributions pushes the combined
figure over it.

## What gets taxed, and at what rate

Division 293 tax is an additional 15% charged on the lesser of the excess over the threshold or
the concessional contributions themselves, on top of the 15% already paid inside the super fund
on those contributions when they were made. The result is that concessional contributions
affected by Division 293 end up taxed at roughly 30% overall rather than 15% — still generally
below most affected taxpayers' marginal tax rate outside super, but a meaningfully different
figure from the headline 15% concessional rate often quoted for super contributions generally.

## It's a separate rule from the concessional contributions cap

Division 293 and the annual concessional contributions cap are independent mechanisms that can
both apply to the same contributions in the same year — a contribution can trigger Division 293
liability, exceed the concessional cap, both, or neither, depending on the taxpayer's combined
income and their total contributions for the year. Being under the concessional cap doesn't mean
Division 293 doesn't apply, and vice versa.

## How it's actually paid

The ATO issues a Division 293 assessment after a tax return is processed, once both income and
contribution information are available. The amount can generally be paid personally or released
from the taxpayer's own super fund using a standard release authority process — it isn't
automatically deducted from the fund without that authority being used.

## Why this matters for timing decisions

Because the test depends on the combined total for the year, income and contribution timing
that shifts either figure — bringing forward or deferring assessable income, or adjusting
salary-sacrifice arrangements — can change whether Division 293 applies at all for a given year,
not just how much is owed. Whether such timing is available, and what its other effects are,
depends on the individual's broader income and super circumstances.

## What this doesn't cover

This describes the Division 293 combined-income test and tax rate only. It doesn't cover the
release authority process in detail, how defined benefit interests are treated differently
under Division 293, or how it interacts with the concessional contributions carry-forward rule
— each depends on the current financial year's published threshold and the taxpayer's specific
super arrangements.
