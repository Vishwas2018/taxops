Contractors — whether working through an ABN, a company structure, or as a PAYG worker with
some self-employed income on the side — are subject to the same general deductibility test as
any other taxpayer. Understanding how that test works, rather than any specific list of
"allowed" items, is what actually determines whether an expense is deductible.

## The general test

The ATO applies three conditions to every deduction claim: the expense must have been paid for
by the taxpayer and not reimbursed, it must be directly related to earning assessable income,
and there must be a record to prove it. All three need to hold — an expense that's work-related
but reimbursed by a client isn't deductible, and neither is an expense with no supporting
record, no matter how clearly work-related it was.

## Apportionment for mixed-use items

Many contractor expenses aren't purely work-related. A laptop, a phone plan, or a home office
might be used for both business and private purposes. In this situation, only the
work-related proportion of the cost is deductible, and that proportion needs a reasonable basis
— a log of work versus private use over a representative period, for example, rather than a
round-number estimate. The private-use portion of a mixed expense is never deductible, however
the item is used.

## Timing: capital versus revenue

Some expenses are deductible immediately (a stationery order, a short-term software
subscription), while others — equipment expected to last more than a year, for instance — are
capital in nature and are instead depreciated over their effective life rather than claimed in
full in the year of purchase. Which treatment applies depends on the nature and cost of the
item, not on which is more convenient in a given year.

## Record-keeping

The ATO's substantiation rules require written evidence for most claims: a receipt, invoice, or
bank statement showing the supplier, the amount, the date, and what was purchased. For
expenses that are partly private, the record also needs to support the work-related
apportionment used. Records generally need to be kept for five years from the date the return
was lodged, since the ATO can request evidence for a claim within that period.

## What this doesn't cover

This is a description of the general framework only. Specific categories — travel between
work sites, a home office, protective equipment — each carry additional eligibility conditions
that this article doesn't detail, and the treatment can depend on how a contractor is
structured (sole trader ABN versus a company). Whether a particular expense meets these
conditions in a specific situation is a question of fact.
