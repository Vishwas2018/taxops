A contractor buying tools, a laptop, or other work equipment faces a timing question the
general deductibility test alone doesn't answer: is the full cost deducted this year, or spread
out over the item's useful life? The answer depends on cost and expected life, not on which
result is more convenient in a given year.

## Immediate deduction versus decline in value

An item costing under a low-value threshold in the year it's bought and used for work can
generally be deducted in full immediately. Above that threshold, an item expected to last more
than twelve months is a depreciating asset — its cost is instead written off over its effective
life using a decline-in-value calculation, with only the current year's portion deductible now
and the rest in future years. The threshold and the immediate-deduction rules can change between
financial years, so the figure that applied last year isn't necessarily this year's figure.

## Effective life and the two calculation methods

Once something is a depreciating asset, its deductible decline in value for the year is worked
out using either the prime cost method (an equal amount each year over the asset's effective
life) or the diminishing value method (a larger amount in earlier years, tapering down). The
ATO publishes effective life determinations for many common asset types, though a taxpayer can
instead self-assess a different effective life if there's a reasonable basis for doing so.

## Low-value pooling

Some depreciating assets below a separate, higher threshold can instead be allocated to a
low-value pool and depreciated together at a single set rate, rather than tracking each asset's
decline in value individually. This mainly simplifies record-keeping for a contractor with many
smaller items rather than changing the total amount ultimately deductible over the group of
assets' combined effective life.

## Work-related use still needs to be apportioned

Everything above assumes 100% work use. A laptop or tool used for both work and private
purposes is depreciated in full, then only the work-related percentage of that year's decline
in value is deductible — the same apportionment principle that applies to any other mixed-use
expense, on a reasonable basis such as a logged usage period.

## Disposal during the asset's effective life

Selling, scrapping, or otherwise disposing of a depreciating asset before its effective life
ends can trigger a balancing adjustment — an extra deduction if the asset was worth less than
its remaining written-down value, or assessable income if it was worth more (for example, a
trade-in value higher than the tax book value). This applies whenever an already-depreciated
asset changes hands or use, not just at the end of its expected life.

## What this doesn't cover

This describes the general immediate-deduction and decline-in-value framework only. It doesn't
cover specific effective-life determinations for particular asset types, the low-value pool's
exact rate and thresholds, or how balancing adjustments interact with an asset that moves
between work and private use over its life — those depend on the specific asset and the current
financial year's published figures.
