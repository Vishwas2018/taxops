Many contractors do at least some work from a home office, whether that's the occasional
invoice and admin session or a full home-based operation. Running costs for that space —
electricity, internet, phone, and similar — are treated differently from a capital home-office
setup like a desk or a computer, and there are two separate methods for working out the
deductible amount.

## The fixed-rate method

The fixed-rate method applies a single cents-per-hour rate to the total hours worked from home
during the year, and that rate is meant to cover electricity, gas, phone and internet usage,
and the decline in value of office consumables like stationery, all in one figure. Only actual
hours worked from home count — the method requires a record of those hours for the whole
financial year (a timesheet, roster, diary, or similar contemporaneous log), not an estimate
based on a typical week. Depreciating assets used for work, like a laptop or monitor, sit
outside the fixed rate and are claimed separately.

## The actual cost method

The alternative is working out the actual work-related proportion of every relevant running
cost individually — a percentage of the home's total electricity bill based on hours and
floor area used for work, for example. This method can produce a different (sometimes higher)
result than the fixed rate, but it requires substantially more record-keeping: bills for every
cost being claimed, plus a reasonable basis for the work-related percentage applied to each
one, such as a floor-area calculation or a representative diary period.

## Only one method applies to a cost per year

The two methods can't be mixed for the same expense in the same year — a contractor using the
fixed rate for a given period can't also separately claim the actual cost of electricity for
that same period, since the fixed rate already includes it. A different method can be used in
different financial years if circumstances change, but each year's claim needs to be internally
consistent.

## A dedicated home office isn't required

Neither method requires a room used exclusively for work — a kitchen table used for invoicing
in the evenings can still support a claim under either method, provided the hours (or floor
area and use pattern, for the actual cost method) are genuinely tracked, not estimated after
the fact.

## Occupancy costs are a separate question

Rent, mortgage interest, council rates, and home insurance are occupancy costs, not running
costs, and are treated under different (and generally more restrictive) rules that mostly apply
only where part of the home has the character of a place of business, not just a convenient
place to work. This article doesn't cover occupancy costs.

## What this doesn't cover

This is a description of the two running-cost methods only. It doesn't cover how to depreciate
a home office asset bought outright, occupancy-cost eligibility, or how these rules interact
with a contractor who also has a separate business premises for part of the year — each depends
on the specific facts of the work arrangement.
