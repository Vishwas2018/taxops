The negative gearing change announced in the 2026-27 Budget is **now law**. The Treasury Laws
Amendment (Tax Reform No. 1) Act 2026 (Act No. 49 of 2026) passed both houses of Parliament on
25 June 2026 and received Royal Assent on 26 June 2026 - it isn't a proposal, and it isn't
waiting on a further vote. What's still ahead is its **commencement**: the substantive change
(Schedule 2 of the Act) takes effect from 1 July 2027, so no return before then is affected, and
current negative gearing rules keep applying exactly as they do today until that date.

## What the Act does, from 1 July 2027

From 1 July 2027, losses on an established (not newly built) residential investment property
purchased from 7:30pm AEST on 12 May 2026 are only deductible against other income from
residential properties, including capital gains on those properties - not against salary, wages,
or other income the way a rental loss can be used today. Where losses exceed that property
income in a given year, the excess carries forward to offset residential property income in
future years rather than being lost.

## Who's grandfathered

Every property held before 7:30pm AEST on 12 May 2026 - including where a contract had been
entered into but not yet settled - keeps access to negative gearing under the current rules for
as long as that property is held. In one sentence: nothing changes for an existing investor's
existing holdings until they sell, and the reform only reaches the tax treatment of established
properties **acquired** from the announcement time onward.

## The transitional window: purchased after announcement, before 1 July 2027

A property purchased between the announcement (7:30pm AEST 12 May 2026) and 30 June 2027 can
still be negatively geared during that window - the new treatment only starts from 1 July 2027
onward, regardless of when the property was purchased within that window.

## New builds are treated differently

A newly constructed residential property that genuinely adds to housing supply - built on
vacant land, or a knock-down rebuild that increases the number of dwellings - keeps access to
negative gearing (and the current 50% CGT discount) both before and after 1 July 2027, for as
long as it hasn't already been sold to a subsequent owner. A knock-down rebuild that doesn't
increase the number of dwellings, a substantial renovation, or a granny flat added to an
established property does not qualify as a new build under this test.

## What the Senate changed before passage

The Bill passed the Senate with amendments rather than in its original form. The one substantive
change likely to matter to a property investor: as a condition of securing the votes needed to
pass, new SMSF limited recourse borrowing arrangements (LRBAs) are now restricted to business
real property - an SMSF can no longer take out a **new** LRBA to acquire residential property.
Existing residential SMSF borrowing arrangements already in place are unaffected by this change.
This is a separate amendment to superannuation borrowing rules, not a negative gearing change
itself, but it affects the same property-investor audience this reform targets.

## What's still pending

The Act is law, but not everything about it is finished. The ATO has not yet published full
guidance and practical tools for applying the new-build test and the loss-carry-forward
mechanics to real transactions - "the Act says X" and "the ATO has published a worked
methodology for X" are different states, and only the first is true right now. Separately, and
**not part of this Act at all**: the Government also announced a 30% minimum tax on
discretionary trusts at the same Budget, proposed to start 1 July 2028 - as of this article's
review date, that measure has not been introduced as a Bill and is not law. Don't conflate the
two: negative gearing reform is enacted; the discretionary trust measure is still just an
announcement.

## What this doesn't cover

This describes the negative gearing change only - the related capital gains tax discount reform
(replacing the 50% discount with cost base indexation and a minimum tax rate) is covered
separately. It doesn't cover commercial property (unaffected), how the changes interact with a
specific ownership structure (individual, trust, company, or partnership), or how carried-forward
losses are tracked and applied in practice - those depend on the specific property and investor
circumstances involved.

## Questions for your registered tax agent

- Does a specific property purchase (or contract date) fall before or after the 7:30pm AEST 12
  May 2026 announcement time?
- Does a planned purchase meet the new build definition, and does that classification survive a
  later sale to a subsequent owner?
- How would carried-forward residential property losses interact with an existing portfolio that
  includes both pre- and post-announcement properties?
- Does an SMSF in the picture have an existing residential LRBA, and does anything change for it
  given the new restriction on **new** arrangements only?
- What ownership structure (individual, trust, company) is being used, and how does the reform's
  scope apply to it specifically?
