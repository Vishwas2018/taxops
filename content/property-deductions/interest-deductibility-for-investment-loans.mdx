Interest is usually the largest single deduction a leveraged property investor has, and it's
also one of the most commonly misunderstood — not because the rule is complicated, but because
it turns on something other than which property secures the loan.

## Purpose of the borrowing, not its security, controls deductibility

The general test is the same one that applies to any deduction: interest is deductible to the
extent the borrowed funds are used to produce assessable income. What the loan is secured
against is irrelevant on its own. A loan secured against an investor's home but used entirely
to buy a rental property can have fully deductible interest, while a loan secured against the
rental property itself but used to fund a private expense — a car, a holiday, a deposit on
what becomes the investor's own home — does not, for the portion used privately, regardless of
the security arrangement.

## Redraw and offset accounts change the analysis

Redrawing extra repayments from an investment loan for a private purpose (a renovation to the
investor's own home, for instance) converts that redrawn portion into private borrowing from
that point on, even though it comes out of what was originally a fully investment-purpose loan.
An offset account attached to the loan doesn't have this effect, since money sitting in an
offset account is never actually redrawn from the loan itself — the loan balance and its
original purpose are unchanged.

## Mixed-purpose loans need apportionment, not an all-or-nothing answer

A single loan used partly for investment and partly for a private purpose isn't wholly
deductible or wholly non-deductible — the interest is apportioned between the two purposes
based on how the funds were actually used, tracked from the point the loan was drawn down.
Where a loan has been progressively repaid and redrawn for different purposes over time, working
out the deductible proportion can require reconstructing the loan's transaction history rather
than relying on the current balance alone.

## Refinancing doesn't reset the purpose test

Refinancing an investment loan to a new lender, or restructuring it, doesn't on its own change
what portion of the debt relates to income-producing use — the original purpose carries across
the refinance. Increasing a loan at refinance time for a private purpose creates the same
mixed-purpose apportionment question as any other private drawdown.

## Records to keep

Loan statements for the full financial year, records of the loan's original purpose and
drawdown amounts, and — for any mixed-purpose or redrawn loan — a reconstruction of which
drawdowns and redraws were investment-related versus private, are what substantiate an interest
deduction if it's ever queried.

## What this doesn't cover

This describes the interest-deductibility purpose test only. It doesn't cover borrowing costs
(loan establishment fees, mortgage insurance) which are treated separately, or how interest
deductibility interacts with a property that changes between investment and private use during
the year — those depend on the specific loan and property history involved.
