A rental property's depreciation deductions come from two entirely separate rules, and the
document that ties them together for a specific property is usually a quantity surveyor's
depreciation schedule, not something an investor works out from a purchase contract alone.

## Division 40: plant and equipment

Division 40 covers depreciating assets within the property — items like carpets, blinds,
hot water systems, and air conditioning units — each with its own effective life and decline
in value calculated separately, the same mechanism used for a contractor's tools and equipment.
A significant restriction applies to residential rental property specifically: plant and
equipment assets that were already installed and used (by a previous owner or occupant) when a
residential property is acquired generally can't have their decline in value deducted by the
new owner at all — this restriction was introduced to stop depreciation being claimed
repeatedly on the same second-hand asset as a property changes hands. Only plant and equipment
the current owner installed new themselves falls outside this restriction.

## Division 43: capital works

Division 43 covers the building's structure and fixed, immovable items — walls, roofing,
plumbing, built-in kitchen cabinetry — deducted at a fixed rate over a long period (typically
several decades) from the date construction was completed, not from the date of purchase. This
deduction is available to the current owner regardless of who built the property, unlike the
Division 40 second-hand restriction above, but only applies to construction that began after
the dates set out in the capital works rules — older buildings may have little or no capital
works deduction available even though the property itself is still standing.

## Why a quantity surveyor's schedule matters

Splitting a property's total cost between land (not depreciable at all), Division 40 assets,
and Division 43 capital works — and then correctly applying the second-hand asset restriction
and the construction-date rules to each component — is a technical costing exercise most
investors aren't positioned to do accurately themselves. A quantity surveyor's report allocates
these amounts using their professional estimate of original construction cost, which the courts
and the ATO both recognise as an acceptable basis for a claim, unlike an investor's own guess at
what the building "must have cost" to build.

## Schedules need updating for later capital additions

A depreciation schedule prepared at purchase doesn't automatically include a later renovation,
a new hot water system, or another capital addition — each significant later addition generally
needs its own costing (either from actual invoices or a further quantity surveyor assessment)
added to the existing schedule, rather than assumed to already be covered by the original
report.

## What this doesn't cover

This describes the Division 40/43 framework and the role of a depreciation schedule only. It
doesn't cover the exact construction-date cutoffs, effective life tables for specific asset
types, or how the second-hand asset restriction applies to a property that was the investor's
own home before becoming a rental — those depend on the specific property's history and the
current financial year's published rules.
