One of the most common areas of confusion for property investors is the line between a
"repair" and an "improvement." The two are treated very differently under tax law, and mixing
them up changes when — and how — a cost can be claimed.

## Repairs: deductible in the year incurred

A repair restores something to its original condition after wear and tear or damage that
happened during the period the property was earning rental income. Replacing a broken section
of guttering, fixing a leaking tap, or repainting a wall damaged by a tenant are typical
examples. Because a repair simply returns the property to its prior state rather than adding
value beyond that, its cost is generally deductible in full in the income year it's paid.

## Improvements: capital in nature

An improvement changes the character of the asset — it makes the property better than it was,
uses substantially different or more modern materials or techniques, or adds something that
wasn't there before. Replacing an entire kitchen with a higher-specification one, or adding a
new deck, are capital works rather than repairs, even though they might be triggered by the
same event (say, storm damage) that would otherwise justify a repair. Capital works costs are
not claimed in full up front; instead they're written off over time under the capital works
deduction rules, typically over several decades depending on the type of work and when it was
completed.

## Initial repairs are a special case

A repair to fix damage that already existed at the time the property was purchased — even if
it looks identical to an ordinary repair — is treated as capital rather than deductible. The
ATO's reasoning is that the cost reflects the condition (and price) of the property at
purchase, not wear and tear that occurred while it was earning rental income for this owner.

## Why the distinction matters for cash flow

Because repairs are deductible immediately and improvements are spread over many years, the
same dollar amount of work can have a very different effect on this year's tax position
depending on which category it falls into. Misclassifying an improvement as a repair
understates capital works and overstates this year's deduction; misclassifying a repair as an
improvement does the opposite.

## What this doesn't cover

This article describes the general repair/improvement distinction only. It doesn't cover
depreciation schedules, quantity surveyor reports, or how partial replacements (like replacing
one damaged section of a larger asset) are assessed — those depend on the specific facts of
the work done, and can be genuinely difficult to classify without a documented assessment.
